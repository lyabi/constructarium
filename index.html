Meaning description: X causes Y to receive Z

Senses

  • Transfer — actual or intended transfer of possession (X causes Y to receive Z)

    She gave him a book.

    Goldberg 1995: 38

  • Enablement — caused enablement of transfer (X enables Y to receive Z)

    She baked him a cake.

  • Promise — intended future transfer via promise (X commits to Y receiving Z)

      About

      Constructarium presents English grammatical constructions for teaching and exploration, with comparison as the primary way into the data.

      What is Construction Grammar?

      Construction Grammar (CxG) is one approach within the wider paradigm of Cognitive Linguistics. At its core is the assumption that language is stored as constructions or "systematic collections of form-function pairings" (Goldberg 2009: 93). Importantly, in accordance with usage-based theory, these constructions are learned through language use and are not part of innate grammar knowledge in the Chomskian sense (Croft 2007: 499-503; Goldberg 2005: 14-15; 2009: 93). Additionally, as a result of the form-meaning fixation, there is no strict division between lexicon and grammar rules but rather a syntax-lexicon continuum (Goldberg & Jackendoff 2004: 532).

      What are Constructions?

      Constructions themselves can be of different form, "including partially or fully filled words, idioms, and general linguistic patterns" (Goldberg 2009: 94). While there is still ongoing discussion as to what constitutes a construction, most theorists agree on the following construction criterion: "Any linguistic pattern is recognized as a construction as long as some aspect of its form or function is not strictly predictable from its component parts or from other constructions recognized to exist" (Goldberg 2005: 5). Additionally, to account for structures which are predictable but still stored separately because of their conventionality, the criterion of "sufficient frequency" (Goldberg 2005: 5) was added.
